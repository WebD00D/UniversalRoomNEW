Art to heart

Jada Fabrizio

Jada Fabrizio says a lot without uttering a word. She speaks through her amazing art. She lives her art. She is her art.

An American Photographer who works in New York City and lives New Jersey, Jada studied creative writing at SUNY New Paltz and photography at the School of Visual Arts in New York.   When commissioned to photograph Haiti to promote tourism, she captured the beautiful beaches and resorts, however she was more compelled to let the camera tell personal stories of the Haitian people. Ever since she has used her camera as a second eye, a narrative tool to tell stories through images.    "By not telling a complete story it allows others to "feel" the photograph in their own way." She goes on to say, "I want the spectator to look at my photographs and experience their own reality through them."

Jada lives the Universal Language Room philosophy––one that encourages living in the moment and letting creativity flow through you, organically and truthfully. It is no wonder Jada and Jason Caplan, founder of the Universal Language Room instantly connected. Jada shared her insights and perspective on the Universal Language Room, and further solidified the legitimacy of people coming together through the great equalizer of music.

How did you meet Jason Caplan?

I met Jason in 2004. He taught guitar to my son, and to me as well for a time. From there I partnered with Jason while he was launching The Universal Language Room. I helped him with graphics, designing a logo, making connections, and photographing his music events and facilitating sessions. It’s funny, we couldn’t be more opposite from an ideological standpoint, but we are the best of friends.

What is your particular philosophy of the power of music?

Music is art. And any art form when done to express and not impress is very therapeutic. I approach my guitar playing as I do my art. Not to be a virtuoso, but to do what comes naturally. It’s a form of meditation. And, oftentimes brilliance springs from that state of relaxation and openness.

What is your perspective of the Universal Language Room?

In the simplest terms it’s a format that brings people together. I’ve photographed some of Jason’s sessions and what I witness is really magical. People come in and they have their guard up. They are hesitant. But Jason’s unbridled enthusiasm is infectious and he’s very open. He really puts them at ease. You physically see them transform. Their shoulders relax, their faces too, you see them let go of inhibitions and step outside themselves. There’s no strict format, rules to adhere to, or “right” way to play the instruments. They just get into it. They really begin to enjoy being in the moment. It’s a freeing organic form of music. It’s as natural as breathing.

Do you use the Universal Language Room philosophy in your own work?

Absolutely. It runs parallel to how I create art. It’s about being automatic in your approach and using what you have to create from the unconscious mind. It’s about stepping outside yourself to make room for other ideas and concepts.

What would you like to see the Universal Room become?

Moving forward I would like to see Jason expand his facilitating to families having problems communicating with each other. Parents with their children, couples, really anyone who needs help communicating with others who are important in their lives.

What Jason does is fun and free of judgment. And when people can have fun and stop having a position they can listen better. The Universal Language Room is a wonderful bridge to other things like marriage and family counseling. Situations where communication is key but verbal communication just isn’t working. I think when people reach an impasse music can help them move forward. I’d like to see sustainable sessions. Not just one or two, but a series so real long-lasting benefits can be realized.
