Positive Percussion Repercussions

Joe Lubman.

Joe Lubman drums for a living. And, not just at gigs in concerts and nightclubs. His talent is hard at work at various theaters, concert halls, schools, community centers, and businesses in the Richmond metro area.


From teaching at Guitar Center Studios and performing in musicals like The Color Purple at the Virginia Repertory Theater, Joe’s skills in drumming and percussion have touched many. TheatreLAB, The Conciliation Project, Art 180, Unity Richmond, Firehouse Theatre (Streetcar named Desire and Wild Party), RAIN (Richmond Autism Integration Network), the list goes on.


What touched me most when speaking with Joe is the time and energy he puts into bringing the healing power of music to those who need it most and the positive outcome of his work as a facilitator for The Universal Room. Read on and you’ll see what I mean.


How did you meet Jason?

Jason needed another drummer for a gig. A mutual friend suggested he contact me. He did and I’ve been playing with him off and on in Naqshon’s Leap for the past three years.


When did you get started drumming?

I started when I was about 12. I took lessons around 7th grade. I played mostly rock and roll until I started studying music at VCU (Virginia Commonwealth University). There I got hooked on jazz.


Why jazz?

Jazz is really complex. If you can play jazz you can play anything. I like achieving that level of proficiency.


Any artists in particular influence you?

Miles Davis and John Coltrane are two of my favorite bandleaders. They produced some of the most ground-breaking albums around. These guys were highly creative and

took risks. The level of commitment to their art definitely had an impact on me.


What is your level of involvement in the Universal Room?

I’m a facilitator. I help people find a voice through music. Jason’s model for a facilitator is someone who guides and coaches, not necessarily teaches and instructs. It’s very informal and free flowing. Participation is encouraged, certainly not enforced. I’m there to provide support and guidance and make people feel comfortable.


Tell me about some of your experiences as a Universal Room facilitator and what came out of it.


For the kids at RAIN (Richmond Autism Integration Network) our sessions are a form of music therapy that helps them deal with some of the challenges of autism. It gives them something to figure out— which they love. It’s sort of like figuring out a puzzle. Communicating can be tough for these kids, so letting the instruments do the talking for them really helps them express themselves as well as connect with peers.


At Art 180, I facilitated weekly sessions for at risk youth. These are the kids that got kicked out school, were having problems at home, and really just needed a place where they could feel accepted and valued. Music does that. And when they have a chance to pick up an instrument they may have never even seen before and play it and express themselves, it gives them a safe and fun outlet for expression. It took a few sessions to break the ice, but these kids really enjoyed it.


Where else can you have a Universal Room session?

Really anywhere. Birthday parties. Corporate team-building events. Any situation where people want to come together, connect and communicate through music.


How does a Universal Room session unfold?

Everyone gets to pick an instrument. Mostly percussion instruments like conga drums, hand drums and shakers, cow bells, that sort of thing. Then they spend an hour playing. At least once in the hour we switch up instruments. If someone likes what they’re playing they don’t have to switch, but most people enjoy trying something new. What’s great about it is there are no rules; no way it has to go down. It’s pretty unstructured, the music and collaboration just sort of unfold naturally. The instruments are easy to play. Anyone can pick one up. There’s no pressure.


What would you like to see the Universal Room become?

I’d like to see it grow and become a national chain of sorts. So no matter where someone is they can go online and see if there’s a Universal Room session going on.



Traci Cox is a freelance writer and music lover from Richmond, Virginia.
