A Leap of Faith

Gilbert Smith dives in.

When Naqshon ben Aminadav walked into the Red Sea before Moses parted the waters, his actions spoke loudly. The water was deep. He would surely drown. But he had faith. There were multitudes behind him. However, it just took this one man’s actions to turn the tides.


Gilbert Smith has faith in positive change. And, he’s making waves.


Co-founder of The Universal Room and one of Jason Caplan’s partners in music, Gilbert Smith is a man of many facets. He’s lived many places from the Tennessee to Illinois to St. Croix. A poet. A songwriter. A rapper. An engineer. Baptized a Christian, Gilbert attended Catholic school, and studied world religions before embracing Bahá’í, the faith that truly spoke to him.


In October of 2006, Jason met Gilbert at the Bahai Center of NYC. Gilbert brought his song writing talents to then just forming Naqshon’s Leap. A fitting name for group of musicians who took a leap of faith to work together and build something that would become greater than the sum of its parts.


Gilbert provides vocals and lyrics. Jason plays guitar and other instruments. Naqshon’s Leap is rounded out by William Ruiz on log and tabloid drums and various other musicians of different faiths and musical backgrounds. The music is a unique synthesis of jazz, rap, funk, country, bluegrass, and rock in one unique sound.


I had a moment to catch up with Gilbert to get his perspective on Naqshon’s Leap and The Universal Room.


What is the inspiration behind Naqshon’s Leap?

People have high ideals about unity, peace and fellowship, but struggle with how to really live it. They want to but don’t know how to get to that place? We strive to make Naqshon’s Leap a vehicle to take people out of their individual silos and put them in a more universal situation. The band goes beyond the idea of music to a more universal way of helping people communicate.


How is Naqshon’s Leap different from other bands?

In addition to playing shows and sharing music, we are always looking for ways to have dialog with people––to go beyond the words and help people speak heart to heart. When we play a show we don’t just pack it up and go home. We operate along the lines of how Broadway and other stage performers do. We go out and meet and mingle with the audience. We interact. Connect. Invite them to participate.



What inspires your song writing?

I try to imagine what it was like for Naqshon when he just dove into the sea. I try to channel that feeling of trust, faith and hope. I use that as inspiration to create and share stories. I’m inspired by the taking the notions of peace and unity and weaving them into a more universal concept that speaks to everyone.



How do you see the Universal Room making an impact?

The Universal Room is not about sitting around hitting drums. It’s a foundation for helping people connect through music, which promotes dialog. It’s a way to reach out and touch someone heart to heart in a non-threatening way.


In many ways the world around us is in a state of discord and imbalance. People are crying out for peace and justice. No matter what faith we are, religion should be a positive force for social change. Ultimately what people are seeking is a connection. A sense of place. The Universal Room can help facilitate that.


How has the Universal Room impacted your journey?

It’s helped validate what I already know. That we as a human race are more similar than we are different. For example, I don’t know any faith that doesn’t have some version of the golden rule.


In Bahá’í we believe all religions have one divine common source. We also believe we have one common universal responsibility: to be a positive force for change. Bahá’í founder, Bahá’u’lláh, said, “incumbent upon every man of insight and understanding is to strive to translate that which has been written into reality and action.” My experience with Universal Room has helped solidify the fact that I have a responsibility to humanity in general. But more importantly it has propelled me past recognition of that fact into action. Going from knowledge to action has been a profound process. I have been able to build some powerful friendships and it’s given me a positive perspective on what the future can hold.


So what does the future hold for The Universal Room?

The players change. The dynamics change. But the vision stays constant. If we can keep that it front of us it will take us to the next place.


I’d like to see The Universal Room ignite some sort of social movement that will make a broader difference for a larger vision of peace and unity.


We hope to inspire people to use their music to create Universal Rooms in other places where people with common interests or circumstances can connect. I’ve seen it work in New York City and now in Richmond. I’ve seen it work in schools, camps, places of worship, corporate settings, you name it.


Because if you can do things on a micro level you see there is hope to do things on a macro level. You just have to dive in.




The Universal Room––where everyone is on the same page.


Traci Cox is a freelance writer and music lover from Richmond, Virginia.
